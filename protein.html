Protein

Protein is an essential macronutrient that plays a crucial role in building and repairing tissues, making enzymes and hormones, and supporting overall health. It is made up of amino acids, which are the building blocks of proteins. There are 20 different amino acids, nine of which are considered essential because the body cannot produce them and must obtain them from food.

  • Amino acids

    In many products, we can find lots of protein, but the most important thing is to know how many amino acids are in the product. Amino acids are the building blocks of protein, and they help with muscle growth, repair, and overall health.
    Let's take an example: nuts are high in protein, sometimes reaching up to 30 grams of protein per 100 grams.
    BUT many meat products also have lots of protein, almost 30 grams of protein per 100 grams (depending on the product). The good thing is that meat has all the amino acids that our body needs, while nuts have only some of them.
    Understanding this, meat products will give you more amino acids than nuts, therefore there will be better muscle growth and repair.
    This doesn't mean that nuts are bad, they still have lots of other nutrients, and the amino acids that they provide are still good for your body.

  • Antinutrients

    Antinutrients are naturally occurring compounds found in many plant-based foods that can interfere with the absorption of nutrients. While they are not harmful in small amounts, consuming large quantities of antinutrients can lead to nutrient deficiencies over time. Examples of antinutrients include phytates, oxalates, and lectins, which can bind to minerals like calcium, iron, and zinc, making them less available for absorption.
    Same goes for protein, to explain it simply, antinutrients can bind to protein and make it less available for absorption. How to stop Antinutrients? To stop antinutrients, you can soak, sprout, ferment, or cook and heat the food. These methods are typically for lentils, beans, nuts, etc.
    For example, soaking beans and lentils before cooking can help reduce the levels of antinutrients, making the protein more bioavailable.

  • Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score (PDCAAS)

    The Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score (PDCAAS) is a method used to evaluate the quality of a protein based on its amino acid composition and digestibility. It is considered the gold standard for assessing protein quality. The PDCAAS score ranges from 0 to 1, with 1 being the highest possible score. A score of 1 indicates that the protein contains all the essential amino acids in the right proportions and is highly digestible.
    Animal-based proteins, such as meat, eggs, and dairy, typically have higher PDCAAS scores compared to plant-based proteins like beans, lentils, and nuts. This is because animal proteins generally contain all the essential amino acids in sufficient quantities and are more easily digested by the body.
    However, it is important to note that consuming a variety of plant-based proteins can help ensure that you get all the essential amino acids your body needs, so make sure to eat a variety of protein sources to meet your nutritional needs.

  • Digestive and Gut Health Issues

    When you eat protein it directly goes to your stomach, where the digestion process starts. But if you have any digestive or gut health issues, it can affect how well your body digests and absorbs protein.
    For example, if you have conditions like irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), Crohn's disease, or celiac disease, your gut may not function properly, therefore the protein digestion and absorption can be compromised, leading to nutrient deficiencies and other health problems.
    There's also something called Enzyme Insufficiency. Here, your body doesn't produce enough digestive enzymes, which are necessary for breaking down food, including protein. This can lead to malabsorption of nutrients and digestive discomfort.
    Intestinal Damage or Malabsorption Syndromes are other issues that can affect protein digestion and absorption. These conditions can damage the lining of the intestines, making it harder for your body to absorb nutrients from food. Keep in mind that the small intestine is where amino acids are absorbed.
    Disruptions to the Gut Microbiome can also affect protein digestion and absorption. The gut microbiome is made up of trillions of bacteria and other microorganisms, but if there's an imbalance in the gut microbiome, it can lead to digestive issues and nutrient deficiencies.

    Genetic and Metabolic Disorders

    There are some cases where people have a metabolic or genetic disorder.
    Amino Acid Transport Disorders: Some genetic conditions, such as Lysinuric Protein Intolerance (LPI), affect the proteins that transport specific amino acids from the intestines into the bloodstream and from the kidneys back into the body. This can lead to a deficiency of these amino acids in the body.
    Urea Cycle Disorders are a group of genetic disorders that affect the body's ability to process and remove waste nitrogen from protein metabolism. This can lead to a buildup of ammonia, which is toxic.
    However, these disorders are rare, so there's no need to worry about them.

    Age-Related Factors

    As we age, our body's ability to digest and absorb protein may decline. This can be due to a variety of factors, including changes in digestive enzyme production, anabolic resistance, and changes in gut health (digestion).
    Also, older adults may also have a reduced appetite, which can lead to lower protein intake and potentially contribute to muscle loss and other health issues.

  • Insufficient Calorie Intake

    If you're not eating enough calories overall, your body may turn protein into energy instead of using it for muscle growth and repair.
    This is because when your body is in a calorie deficit, it will prioritize using protein for energy rather than for other functions. However, if you're on a calorie deficit, and if you eat enough protein, your body will still use some of that protein for muscle growth and repair. Also, your body will use fat for energy.
    So make sure to eat enough calories overall, with enough protein.

    Stress

    Chronic stress can affect your body's ability to digest and absorb protein properly.
    When you're stressed, your body releases hormones like cortisol, which can interfere with muscle growth and repair.
    Also, stress can affect your gut health, leading to digestive issues that can impact protein absorption.
    So make sure to manage your stress levels through relaxation techniques, good sleep, and good exercise.
